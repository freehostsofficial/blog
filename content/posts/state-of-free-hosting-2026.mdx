## The Big Picture

Free hosting continues its upward trajectory. More platforms, more generous limits, and better developer experience across the board. The competition between Cloudflare, Vercel, Netlify, and newer entrants has been a rising tide that lifts all boats.

## What's New

### Cloudflare's Expanded Free Tier

Cloudflare continued to invest heavily in their free tier throughout 2025. Pages now supports larger build outputs, and Workers free tier got a bump to 100,000 requests per day. The addition of D1 (their SQL database) to the free tier was the biggest surprise.

### Vercel's Pricing Adjustments

Vercel made some controversial changes to their pricing in late 2025, but the free tier remained largely intact. The hobby plan still includes 100 GB bandwidth and unlimited static deployments. The main change was clearer limits on serverless function execution time.

### The Rise of Railway and Render

Railway and Render both expanded their free tiers to attract individual developers. While neither matches Cloudflare's unlimited bandwidth, both offer more flexibility for full-stack applications that need a database and background workers.

## Platforms We Lost

Not every platform survived 2025. Heroku's free tier, already on life support, was finally discontinued entirely. Several smaller platforms like Surge and Now (the original Vercel) were either sunset or absorbed into their parent companies.

## Looking Ahead

The trend is clear: free hosting is becoming more capable, not less. The platforms that survive are the ones that use free tiers as a genuine acquisition channel, not a charity. This means the free tier has to be good enough for real projects, and in 2026, it absolutely is.

## Our Recommendations

For static sites, start with Cloudflare Pages. For full-stack applications, try Railway or Render. For Next.js specifically, Vercel remains the path of least resistance. And for anything experimental, the free tier of any major cloud provider will get you surprisingly far.
