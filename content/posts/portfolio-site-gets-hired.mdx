## Beyond the Template

Every junior developer has a portfolio site. Most look identical — a hero section with a wave emoji, three project cards, and a contact form that sends to nowhere. If you want to stand out, you need to think differently about what a portfolio actually is.

A portfolio isn't a résumé in HTML form. It's a demonstration of your ability to **design, build, and ship** something real. The site itself is the project.

## What Hiring Managers Actually Look For

After interviewing dozens of engineering managers, a clear pattern emerged. They care about three things when evaluating portfolio sites:

1. **Does the site work?** Broken links, slow loads, and layout bugs are immediate red flags.
2. **Is there evidence of real work?** Tutorial projects don't count. They want to see problems you identified and solved.
3. **Can you write?** Case studies and blog posts demonstrate communication skills, which matter as much as code.

## The Technical Foundation

Keep your stack simple. A static site generator with markdown content is all you need. Next.js with static export, Astro, or even plain HTML — the technology matters less than the execution.

Host it for free on Cloudflare Pages or Vercel. Use a custom domain. Make sure it loads fast on mobile. These basics matter more than any animation library.

## Content That Matters

Write about your projects. Not just what they do, but why you built them, what went wrong, and what you learned. This is the content that hiring managers actually read. A well-written case study is worth more than a dozen project cards with GitHub links.

## Ship It

The biggest mistake is waiting until your portfolio is "perfect." Ship it now, iterate later. A live site with three solid projects beats a local development server with twenty unfinished ones.
